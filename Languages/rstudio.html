1.4 stats

1.4.1 Binomial distributions

dbinom(k,n,p)= \(P(Binim(n,p)=k)\) or how likely it is to get \(k\) successes from \(n\) trials with \(p\) probabilty of success

pbinom(q,n,p) returns P(Binom(n,p)≤q) or (the probability \(p\) of the random variable being \(q\) or less in \(n\) number of trials)
to compute the opposite 1-pbinom(q,n,p) or pbinom(q,n,p,lower.tail=F) (this is P(X>q)** not **P(X≥q))
qbinom(pr,n,p) tells you how large \(q\) needs to be for P(Binom(n,p)≤q) to be at least \(pr\), (it is the inverse of pbinom(q,n,p))

1.4.2 Normal distributions

dnorm(x,mu,sigma)=the height of the density function \(N(\mu,\sigma)\) at x
pnorm(q,mu,sigma) returns P(X≤q) and is the same as P(X<q) to compute the opposite 1-pnorm(q,mu,sigma) or pnorm(q,mu,sigma,lower.tail=F)
qnorm(pr,mu,sigma) tells you how large \(q\) needs to be for P(norm(mu,sigma)≤q) to be at least \(pr\), (it is the inverse of pnorm(q,mu,sigma))

1.4.3 Statistical Inference

qnorm(C + (1-C)/2) * SE or another q___ function calculates the margin of error

cor(x,y) or cor(y,x) (they are the same) calculates the correlation coefficient \(r\) between two vectors

lm(y~x) where y is the response variable and x is the predictor variable (MUST be in this order)
coef(df) can be used to just pull the point estimates for the intercept and slope
resid(df) can be used to extract the residuals
predict(lm(y~x), newdata = df(x)) can predict the \(\hat{Y}\) values optional addition interval = "prediction" or "confidence"

t.test(data, conf.level(default .95)) takes in a vector of raw data and returns a confidence interval and output from a hypothesis test.
